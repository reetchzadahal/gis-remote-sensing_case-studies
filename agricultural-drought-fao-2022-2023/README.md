# Agricultural Drought Assessment using VCI (FAO Case Study: 2022–2023)

## Overview

This case study presents an analysis of agricultural drought using satellite-based remote sensing data published by the Food and Agriculture Organization (FAO).

The study focuses on drought severity and exposure of rainfed and irrigated agricultural areas, with a comparative perspective across multiple years.


## Objectives

* Assess agricultural drought using Vegetation Condition Index (VCI)
* Identify drought-affected areas (VCI < 40%)
* Analyze exposure of:

  * Rainfed agriculture
  * Irrigated agriculture
* Understand implications for food security and climate resilience


## Study Context

The analysis is based on FAO-supported drought assessment work conducted for Afghanistan under the Emergency Food Security Project.

A key motivation of this study is to explore how similar approaches can support drought monitoring and planning in countries like Nepal.


## Data and Tools

* MODIS satellite data (VCI)
* Land cover data from ICIMOD (2018)
* GIS and Remote Sensing techniques


## Methodology

* Monthly VCI data (January–April) combined to represent growing season
* Classification into five drought severity classes:

  * Extreme, Severe, Moderate, Mild, No drought
* Spatial analysis using:

  * Overlay of drought and agricultural land
  * Hexagonal grid mapping for visualization


## Key Findings

### Rainfed Agriculture

* Highly vulnerable to drought conditions
* Significant increase in extreme drought exposure in some regions
* Western and northern areas most affected

### Irrigated Agriculture

* More resilient than rainfed systems
* Still significantly impacted under severe drought
* Increasing drought exposure observed over time

### Drought Severity

* Large proportion of land affected by moderate to extreme drought
* Spatial concentration of severe drought in northern and western regions
* Year-to-year increase highlights worsening conditions


## Relevance to Nepal

This study highlights the importance of agricultural drought monitoring in Nepal, where:

* A large proportion of the population depends on agriculture
* Climate variability is increasing drought frequency

Remote sensing and GIS-based drought assessment can support:

* Early warning systems
* Food security planning
* Climate adaptation strategies


## Limitations

* Lack of ground validation data
* Dependence on satellite data quality
* Limited temporal scope (short analysis period)
